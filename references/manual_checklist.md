# Manual Checklist

These 7 items cannot be verified from the LinkedIn data export. Present them to the user as a guided review after completing the automated analysis.

Each "yes" adds points to the final score (weighted by importance). Maximum bonus: +12 points on top of the 88-point automated scale.

For each "no" answer, provide the specific recommendation listed below.

Items are ordered by impact — address them in this order.


## 1. Open to Work (+3 points)

**Question:** Have you configured the "Open to Work" preferences with specific job titles, locations, and employment types you're targeting?

**Why it matters:** This is the single highest-impact setting for job seekers. LinkedIn's "Open to Work" feature surfaces your profile to recruiters with matching roles. Without it configured, you're invisible to this entire discovery channel. Recruiters actively filter by candidates who have this enabled.

**If no:** Go to your profile → "Open to" button → "Finding a new job":
- Add 1-3 specific job titles you're targeting (use the exact titles recruiters search for)
- Set your preferred locations (or "Remote")
- Choose employment types (Full-time, Contract, etc.)
- Set visibility to "Recruiters only" if you don't want your current employer to see
- Review and update these preferences monthly — stale preferences reduce match quality


## 2. Profile Photo (+2 points)

**Question:** Do you have a professional headshot with good lighting, a neutral background, and industry-appropriate attire?

**Why it matters:** Profiles with professional photos get significantly more views. A clear headshot builds trust and makes recruiters more likely to engage.

**If no:** Get a photo with these characteristics:
- Head and shoulders framing
- Neutral or simple background (no clutter)
- Good lighting (natural light or professional)
- Appropriate attire for your industry (business casual for tech is fine)
- No group photos, selfies, or vacation shots
- No heavy filters or editing


## 3. Privacy and Visibility Settings (+2 points)

**Question:** Have you verified that your profile is visible to recruiters (public profile enabled, not restricted to connections only)?

**Why it matters:** If your profile visibility is restricted, recruiters literally cannot find you in search results regardless of how good your content is.

**If no:** Check these settings:
- Settings → Visibility → Profile viewing options: choose "Your name and headline"
- Settings → Visibility → Who can see your connections: "Anyone" or "Connections"
- Settings → Data Privacy → Review if "AI Improvement" settings match your preferences
- Ensure your profile is set to "Public" for maximum discoverability


## 4. Recommendations (+2 points)

**Question:** Do you have at least 2 recommendations from diverse sources (managers, peers, reports) that mention specific skills and achievements?

**Why it matters:** Recommendations are social proof. They build credibility with recruiters who are evaluating whether your self-reported accomplishments are real. Quality and recency matter more than quantity.

**If no — and you want to improve this:**
- Reach out to 2-3 former colleagues or managers who can speak to specific projects
- Make it easy: suggest what they might highlight (a project, a skill, a result)
- Offer to write one for them in exchange (reciprocity works)
- Prioritize recommendations from people with recognizable titles or companies
- Recent recommendations (within 2-3 years) carry more weight than old ones

**Important:** This is a "nice to have" — not having recommendations is not a red flag. Recruiters care far more about your headline, skills, and experience descriptions. Only invest time here after the higher-priority items are addressed.


## 5. Featured Section (+1 point)

**Question:** Do you have at least one item in your Featured section (article, portfolio link, GitHub repo, presentation, or certification)?

**Why it matters:** The Featured section is proof of work. Recruiters appreciate tangible evidence of your skills beyond text descriptions.

**If no:** Add at least one of these:
- Link to your GitHub profile or a standout repository
- A technical blog post or article you've written
- A conference talk or presentation
- A portfolio or personal website
- A notable certification badge


## 6. Custom URL (+1 point)

**Question:** Is your LinkedIn URL customized (e.g., linkedin.com/in/yourname) without random numbers or characters?

**Why it matters:** Recruiters copy-paste URLs into ATS systems. A clean URL looks professional and is easier to share on resumes and portfolios.

**If no:** Go to Settings → Public profile → Edit your custom URL. Use your name or professional brand (e.g., linkedin.com/in/johnsmith-dev). Avoid nicknames.


## 7. Cover Photo (+1 point)

**Question:** Do you have a cover photo that is high-resolution, relevant to your industry or personal brand, and free of poorly positioned text?

**Why it matters:** The cover photo is the largest visual element on your profile. A relevant, professional banner strengthens your brand and makes the profile visually complete.

**If no:** Options for a good cover photo:
- A clean graphic related to your field (code symbols, architecture diagrams, tech patterns)
- Your company or community branding
- A cityscape of where you're based (for location signaling)
- Use Canva's free LinkedIn banner templates for quick professional results
- Avoid: blurry images, vacation photos, text that's cut off on mobile
